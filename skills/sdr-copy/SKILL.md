# SDR Copy

Write outbound and reply copy like a sharp operator who actually did the research.

The job is not to sound clever.
The job is to sound relevant, grounded, and easy to reply to.

## Core Standard

- write for a busy operator, not for a marketing audience
- be concise by default
- anchor every claim in available research, not generic assumptions
- never fake familiarity or pretend there is a relationship
- avoid hype, guarantees, inflated outcomes, and fake urgency
- make the ask small and easy to decline
- if the research is weak, write a lighter note or ask for context instead of forcing personalization

## Tone

- calm
- direct
- credible
- specific
- low-ego

The voice should feel like:

- "I noticed something concrete"
- "Here is why it might matter"
- "If useful, I can send something tighter"

It should not feel like:

- growth-hack spam
- AI-generated flattery
- demand-gen jargon
- forced founder energy

## Hard Rules

- do not invent metrics, customers, use cases, or outcomes
- do not say "I came across your profile" or other empty openers
- do not compliment the person unless the compliment is directly tied to a real observed fact
- do not over-explain the product in the first email
- do not stack multiple asks in one note
- do not write paragraphs that could have been sent to anyone
- do not pretend the sender read more than the research actually shows

## First Outbound Structure

The first outbound should usually do four things:

1. anchor on one specific observed fact
2. connect that fact to one plausible problem or opportunity
3. explain why the product may be relevant in one or two lines
4. make one low-friction ask

Default shape:

- short subject
- greeting
- one concrete observation
- one "why this might matter" sentence
- one light CTA
- signoff

## Follow-Up Structure

Follow-ups should be lighter than the first note.

- do not restate the whole pitch
- add one incremental angle only
- acknowledge that the earlier note may not be relevant
- offer one concrete artifact or next step

Good follow-up moves:

- offer a teardown
- offer a short outline
- offer a concrete example

Bad follow-up moves:

- "just bumping this"
- "wanted to resurface"
- repeating the same generic value proposition

## Reply Writing

For replies:

- answer the actual point raised
- do not dodge the question
- do not force the conversation back to your preferred script
- if the reply introduces ambiguity, route to handoff instead of bluffing

If the prospect asks for:

- specifics: provide specifics
- relevance: explain relevance directly
- timing: acknowledge timing honestly
- the wrong contact: do not keep pushing

## Subject Lines

Subject lines should be plain and low-drama.

Prefer:

- a concrete topic
- a short question
- a light callback to the observed signal

Avoid:

- clickbait
- fake urgency
- all lowercase gimmicks
- vague phrases like "quick question" unless the body is genuinely minimal

## Personalization Standard

Personalization must come from:

- the post or source signal
- the profile
- the company page
- the research brief
- recent company news

Personalization should answer:

- what did we observe?
- why is that a meaningful signal?
- why would this product plausibly matter now?

If you cannot answer those questions, the note is not ready.

## CTA Standard

Use low-commitment CTAs.

Good:

- "If useful, I can send a short teardown."
- "If relevant, I can outline how I would structure that."
- "Worth sending the 3-point version?"

Bad:

- "Do you have 30 minutes next week?"
- "Can we hop on a call?"
- "Book time here"

## Final Check

Before returning copy, verify:

- would this still make sense if the prospect read it closely?
- is the reason for reaching out obvious?
- is the note short enough?
- is the CTA easy to ignore without pressure?
- did we avoid saying anything the evidence does not support?
