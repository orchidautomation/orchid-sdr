# Research Brief

Produce research briefs that are useful for qualification, copywriting, and handoff.

This is not a generic summary skill.
This is an operator research skill.

## Goal

Turn raw signal research into a concise brief that answers:

- who this person is
- what company they are at
- what signal was observed
- why this could matter now
- what the likely angle is for outreach
- what is still uncertain

## Required Inputs

Use as many of these as are available:

- the source post or signal
- the author profile
- the company page
- recent company news
- the active `knowledge/icp.md`

## Required Sequence

1. Confirm the person and company identity.
2. Read the source signal closely.
3. Pull the strongest company context.
4. Check for recent news or timing context if useful.
5. Compare the evidence to the ICP.
6. Write a brief that is useful for the next downstream action.

## What The Brief Must Cover

The summary should usually cover five things in this order:

1. Fit
   - why this person/company appears relevant or not
2. Signal
   - what happened that made them enter the funnel
3. Why now
   - timing, change, initiative, hiring, launch, funding, or operational pressure
4. Outreach angle
   - the most credible angle for a first note
5. Risks or unknowns
   - what is still weak, missing, or speculative

## Required Copy Guidance

Every brief should also produce explicit downstream copy guidance:

- `primaryAngle`
- `bestOpeningHook`
- `whyNow`
- `avoidMentioning`
- `ctaSuggestion`

These fields are for the drafter, not for human theatrics.

They should answer:

- what is the strongest real angle?
- what should the opener anchor on?
- what timing context is actually credible?
- what should we avoid saying even if it is technically true?
- what low-friction CTA fits this lead?

The goal is to reduce improvisation in copy generation.
The copy layer should not need to reverse-engineer the whole brief every time.

## Summary Standard

Write the summary so a human operator could glance at it and immediately know:

- whether this lead is interesting
- what the pitch angle should be
- what evidence supports that angle
- what might be wrong about the current read

Do not write a wall of text.
Do not write a marketing pitch.

## Evidence Standard

Evidence should be:

- concrete
- attributable
- short
- actually useful

Prefer evidence items that point to:

- the original post
- the profile
- the company site
- a relevant news item

Each note should explain why the source matters, not just repeat the title.

## Confidence Standard

Confidence should reflect:

- identity clarity
- evidence quality
- ICP fit strength
- timing clarity
- contradiction risk

Use lower confidence when:

- there is only one weak signal
- the company context is thin
- the outreach angle depends on too much inference

## Good Brief Behavior

Good briefs are:

- compact
- evidence-led
- commercially useful
- honest about uncertainty
- specific enough that a drafter can write from them without reinterpreting the whole research set

Bad briefs are:

- generic
- padded
- speculative
- written like internal fluff
